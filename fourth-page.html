Mental health and social media

The importance

While social media in and of itself is not a bad thing-- it can be used to keep in touch long distance, get news, see what’s going on with old friends-- too much social media can have adverse mental health impacts. A study from PubMed.gov found that higher social media usage is more likely to be associated with depression in young adults. Studies such as this one suggest that, while social media can be a helpful tool, passive social media users experience better mental health than active social media users.

The social media detox

A social media detox is intentionally abstaining from social media to improve one’s mental health. The amount of time one does a social media detox for is up to them, but it’s typically completed in 30 days. The goal of a social media detox is to gain back some of the mental energy one expels each time they visit a social media platform. One article suggests that a social media detox allows a person to clear their mind, take back control of their digital habits, gain more free time and experience better mornings and less anxiety.

Steps for doing a social media detox are pretty self-explanatory.

Tell people what you're going to be doing so it’s easier to hold yourself accountable.

Plan what you’ll do doing your detox instead of spending time on social media.

Keep in touch with your friends in family during the detox so you’ll still feel in the loop!

Start the detox!

Healthy social media practices

  1. Limit the time you spend scrolling each day
  2. Focus on your real-life friends
  3. Follow pages and people that bring you joy
  4. Avoid using social media and your phone before bed
